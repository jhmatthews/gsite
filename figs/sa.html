Improving our Stellar Atmosphere Models

Quite a lot of discussion recently has been devoted to what stellar atmosphere models to use for our disk treatment and why. This is important in the CV context in understanding the Balmer jump, and also for any effect on ionization. It also has wider ranging implications for e.g. Lyman edges in QSOs and similar applications.

Originally, I'd run my CV models with Kurucz stellar atmosphere models for each annulus. There were a number of initial bugs (see 67), and also a few problems associated wiht the models themselves
  • The temperatures only go up to 50,000K - this means that for the inner disk we have to take a 50000K atmosphere and scale it up in each frequency bin by [exp(hnu/k*50000) - 1] / [exp(hnu/kT) - 1] (BB function scaling)
  • the resolution of the Kurucz models is not great
  • They also have wavelength limits
The proposal is to use spectra generated from Tlusty stellar atmosphere calculations that then use Synspec to produce the actual spectra for each atmosphere (and we use Rotin to, but exactly what for I'm not sure?- see also the bottom of this page for note on normalising Tlusty models).

We will need to extend the spectra at both long and short wavelengths. if we make the assumption that at long wavelengths the spectrum will have recovered to the blackbody shape pretty well (which we need to test!) then we have three options that I can think of at the Wien end of a spectrum of temp T
  • extrapolate using the temperature T (would lead to a discontinuity AND an incorrect flux, due to the redistribution of flux over various wavelengths by the atmosphere)
  • extrapolate using an effective temperature T1, calculated such that the total flux adds up to sigma*T^4. This would lead to a discontinuity, but have the total flux correct.
  • extrapolate using an effective temperature T2, calculated from a blackbody that would overlap the last bit of the tail from the stellar atmosphere. This would lead to no discontinuity but would have an incorrect flux.
I am going to try all of these out and test, and put the results here. We want to test how much the flux can even change (are we always making a negligible difference with all these choices?). We will also do rough comparisons to Kurucz to check it all looks sensible.

Another pertinent question is whether this choice of disk model in the ionization cycles makes much difference to the output spectrum. Figure 1 and Figure 2 show a comparison between using a BB in the ionization stages or a Kurucz model. A Kurucz model is used in the spectral cycles in both cases.

KSL's notes on normalisation of TLUSTY

The normalization of Tlusty is given by:

R= D sqrt(N/(4pi))

where N=f_observed/f_Synspec (so for a distance of 44 pc, WZ Sge R=3.8229e19*sqrt(N)

Boris' explanation of why this is right:

I use

f        Rwd2
- = 4pi  -----
H        d2

with f the observed flux, H the model flux, Rwd the white dwarf radius and d the distance. Ivan's models are in Eddington flux (H), just as my own pure-H models. [Extra care is necessary when comparing with other models - e.g. Koester uses the "astrophysical flux" in his models, which is defined as F=4H].


Ivan's explanation
4pi should be there; this is because of the relation between H and physical flux; 2 is because H is defined as (1/2)\int d\mu, and 2pi because one has also to integrate over the other angle, phi, from 0 to 2pi; the intensity in a 1-D case is independent of phi. (See also Mihalas, Stellar Atmospheres 2nd ed, eq (1-27)


Note in terms of H, the luminosity of a star is

L=16 pi**2 Rwd**2 H (so there is an extra factor of 4pi)

A few things to note

  • the fact that sigma*T^4 is e.g. actually slightly less than my BB is just from accuracy of my integrating- I just summed up binsize by bin value and so am limited by the resolution of the Kurucz model there.
  • Clearly we are missing a fairly reasonable chunk of flux in the disk14 models and so will need to be careful with the extrapolation
  • Note the differences at the UV end- do we have any suspected reasons for this? Just fundamental differences in the atmosphere physics the codes do?
  • Also, the Tlusty/disk14 models have a fair amount of noise- the one plotted above actually has a smoothing factor of 11 applied to aid comparison. It seems to me that we are not gaining that much in some cases by improving the resolution of the models as a lot of it seems to be pretty noisy. However, note that lines all look pretty good.

Extending the Models

I've been looking at the best way to extend the models. I've tried three approaches, none of which really produce the right amount of flux so I may try the other option of fixing the temperature for the extrapolation so as to reproduce sigmaT^4. Here's an an example of my various different extrapolations, for 90000K, log_g = 5.

Figure 4:


Here there are three types of extrapolation tried, and the original KSL model is in cyan:

bb extrap: literally just tag a BB with the temperature of the model on to both ends (red, big discontinuity in this case).

cont: Do an extrapolation by working out what temperature a BB curve would have to have to pass through the end of the model, and use that temperature (every point in wavelength-flux space has a unique temperature, use the one corresponding to the end value) - (blue)

cont 2: Do an extrapolation by using the temperature of the model, but apply a correction factor which scales the BB body curve on to the ends of the model curve (dotted black).

You can see the models from 10000K to 90000K in the folder ~/Dropbox/Python/James/disk14/- It's interesting to see where the different flux emerges depending on temperature.

It's a little confusing why none of the models actually reproduce sigma*T**4, I was expecting them to with the extrapolation. The Kurucz models DO produce sigma T**4.





Comparison with Pickles Atlas

In the telecon on 140314, KSL suggested looking at the Pickles Stellar Spectral Flux library (webpage here). Here's a comparison between a 9500 K model, one from the Pickles library which is actually an A0V spectral type with T_eff of 9549.43. I normalised the Pickles one to sigma*T**4, so we should only really pay attention to the spectral shape rather than the area under the curve (although we already know that the Kurucz models will sum up correctly whereas Hubeny models won't).

Figure 5


The Pickles and Kurucz models produce much more similar spectral shapes than the Hubeny models. Coupled with the flux problem, this definitely suggests to me that there is something wrong with either the TLUSTY or SYNSPEC portion of the simulation which not only leads to incorrect flux, but also incorrect shape, and thus the Kurucz models are currently a better physical model.

I think we need to either work out what the problem is with the TLUSTY/Hubeny models or simulate Kurucz models at finer resolution using SYNSPEC. this second step will also help us understand where the spectrum is going wrong.


190314- Plot of disk properties

Figure 6
Knox had asked me to see where on the disk the optical and UV flux was emerging (at least I think that's what he meant). The claim was that ~90% of the light arises very close to the star, which does seem to be approximately true in the UV (800-2000), but not so much in the optical (3000-7000). The figure above shows, top to bottom

temperature, log_g, UV cumulative luminosity, Optical cumulative luminosity, full range(0.1-200000A).

I think what this shows, really, is that what will really be affected by the inaccurate SA models beyong 50,0000K is more the *ionizing* flux, rather than what we actually see in the optical. This is still clearly important, due to e.g. He II, but I think this means that it won't have all that much affect on the Optical shape. This is perhaps unsurprising, as even at 10000K the peak of a BBody is at ~2400A, blueward of 3000, so I think the increased area of the cooler region compensates for the increase in optical flux, because we are on the RJ tail (ish).

Just to be clear- I'm not suggesting this means Kurucz models are fine to use, and clearly if I changed the mass loss rate this conclusion might change significantly.
